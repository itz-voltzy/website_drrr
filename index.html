Exposure

Exposure refers to who and what are located in areas at risk of earthquakes are the people, built environment, infrastructure, natural systems, economic assets, and other elements that are present in earthquake hazard zones and therefore subject to potential loss or damage if seismic events occur. In earthquake risk assessments, exposure is quantified by identifying the inventory of elements that might be affected including populations, homes, schools, hospitals, bridges, utilities, and other critical infrastructure in seismically active areas. Studying exposure helps planners understand the scale of impact that an earthquake could have before considering how vulnerable these elements are or what capacities exist to respond and recover. For example, exposure models can detail the number and type of buildings at risk and the socioeconomic characteristics of populations living near fault zones.

Vulnerablity

Vulnerability describes the susceptibility or likelihood of harm that exposed elements (people, structures, systems) have when affected by an earthquake. It captures the intrinsic weaknesses physical, social, economic, institutional that can magnify damage and losses. In disaster literature, vulnerability is defined as the conditions determined by factors that increase susceptibility to hazard impacts, including poor infrastructure, lack of preparedness, social inequality, limited access to resources, and weak governance. It’s not just exposure to the hazard itself, but the capability (or incapability) to anticipate, cope with, resist, and recover from the event.

Capacities

Capacities are the strengths, resources, and abilities that individuals, communities, organizations, and governments have to anticipate, prepare for, respond to, and recover from earthquakes. It’s the positive counterpart to vulnerability the elements that reduce risk and enhance resilience. Strong capacities help reduce vulnerability and limit the consequences of an earthquake by enabling effective preparedness, rapid response, and sustainable recovery. Fostering capacity through education, infrastructure improvements, policies, and community engagement is a central goal of disaster risk reduction strategies.
